# Cuculi Content Calendar — March 6–31, 2026

> **Goal**: Execute four themed content weeks, each targeting a different audience segment with a distinct content format, to generate baseline performance data and identify what resonates before scaling in Q2.

-----

## Calendar Overview

This calendar follows the **“one idea → many surfaces”** repurposing model. Each week anchors on a single blog post that feeds the newsletter, 2–3 social clips, and in-app Leo messaging. Weeks are deliberately varied by theme, audience, funnel stage, and content format so you can compare performance across dimensions — not just topics.

|Week|Dates    |Theme                         |Primary Audience                   |Funnel Stage |Blog Format           |
|----|---------|------------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------|----------------------|
|1   |Mar 6–12 |Late Winter Reactivation      |Inactive Users                     |Activation   |First-Person Narrative|
|2   |Mar 13–19|The Culture Shift             |Prospective Users (SEO/Social)     |Awareness    |Trend Think-Piece     |
|3   |Mar 20–26|Spring Kickoff: What to Expect|New Browsers / First-Timers        |Consideration|Transparency Guide    |
|4   |Mar 27–31|Community Spotlight           |Frequent Users & Community Builders|Retention    |Host/Member Profile   |

-----

## Week 1: Late Winter Reactivation (Mar 6–12)

**Theme**: Get lapsed users off the sidelines before spring arrives.
**Primary Audience**: Inactive users (downloaded but never attended, or lapsed 3–6+ months).
**Goal**: Drive RSVPs from dormant users. Target 5%+ click-through to event pages.
**Psychology**: Empathy → identification → low-pressure invitation.

### Content Pieces

**Blog (Publish Monday Mar 9)**
_“I Had the App for Months and Never Went — Here’s What a Tuesday Night Changed”_

A first-person reactivation narrative (800–1,200 words) modeled on the highest-rated headline format from the psychological content strategy. The story follows the arc of download → stall → one spontaneous Tuesday → relief. Ends with a soft CTA to browse upcoming tables.

- **SEO Keywords**: “social dining app NYC,” “how to meet people in NYC”
- **Internal Links**: Link to 2–3 upcoming March tables
- **Format**: Story/Experience template

**Newsletter (Send Wednesday Mar 11)**

- Lead: Excerpt hook from the blog (“The hardest part wasn’t the dinner — it was the 6 months before it.”)
- Featured Events: 2–3 upcoming tables within the next 10 days, selected for low barrier (small group size, casual venues, nearby neighborhoods)
- Segment: Send to inactive cohort only
- Subject Line A/B Test: “You downloaded the app. Here’s what comes next.” vs. “One Tuesday night changed everything.”

**Social (3 posts across the week)**

1. _Instagram Carousel (Tue)_: “5 things that kept me from going to my first dinner — and why none of them mattered.” Visual, slide-by-slide myth-busting format.
2. _TikTok/Reels (Thu)_: 30-second clip — “POV: You finally go to the dinner you’ve been avoiding for 6 months.” Low-fi, relatable, humor-forward.
3. _Twitter/X (Fri)_: Text post — “The gap between downloading a social dining app and actually going to dinner is… weirdly emotional. Anyone else sit on it for months?”

**In-App Leo Message**
Target: Users inactive 60+ days.
Message: “Spring tables are filling up — and there’s a casual one near you this week. Here’s a post about what to expect: [blog link]”

### Rationale

This is the format your strategy identified as having the highest psychological conversion potential for the largest user segment. First-person narrative directly addresses the emotional objection (“I downloaded but never went”) and lowers the perceived risk. Running this in Week 1 establishes the reactivation baseline early so you can measure lift across the month.

### What You’re Testing

- Does first-person narrative outperform other formats for click-through?
- Does the inactive-user newsletter segment respond to empathy-driven subject lines?
- Which social format (carousel vs. video vs. text) drives the most profile taps?

-----

## Week 2: The Culture Shift (Mar 13–19)

**Theme**: Position Cuculi as part of a larger cultural moment, not just a product.
**Primary Audience**: Prospective users discovering Cuculi via search and social.
**Goal**: Top-of-funnel reach and new sign-ups. Target 25K+ impressions, 2%+ social engagement rate.
**Psychology**: Trend validation + social proof. Readers see their own dissatisfaction reflected back as a cultural movement.

### Content Pieces

**Blog (Publish Monday Mar 16)**
_“Group Dinners Are Quietly Replacing Dating Apps — Here’s Why It’s Working”_

A trend think-piece (1,500–2,000 words) that positions social dining as a generational shift away from swipe culture. References broader loneliness data, the “third place” concept, and the rise of IRL community platforms. Cuculi is mentioned as an example — not as the pitch. Designed for SEO and shareability.

- **SEO Keywords**: “group dinners replacing dating apps,” “social dining trend,” “making friends as an adult NYC”
- **External Sources**: Cite loneliness research (Surgeon General’s advisory), third-place sociology, relevant trend coverage
- **Format**: Standard blog article template with thought-leadership angle

**Newsletter (Send Wednesday Mar 18)**

- Lead: “Something interesting is happening at dinner tables across the city…” (tease the blog’s thesis)
- Featured Events: 2–3 tables positioned as “the kind of night people are choosing instead of another dating app”
- Segment: Full list (all subscribers) — this is awareness content, not segment-specific
- Subject Line A/B Test: “Why strangers are choosing dinner over dating apps” vs. “The social shift no one’s talking about”

**Social (3 posts across the week)**

1. _Instagram Reel (Mon)_: 60-second “hot take” video — “Dating apps peaked. Here’s what’s replacing them.” Hook-driven, text overlay, trending audio.
2. _LinkedIn Post (Wed)_: Professional angle — “The ‘third place’ is back, and it looks like a dinner table.” Written for the 25–40 professional audience. Link to blog.
3. _TikTok (Fri)_: Stitch/duet format responding to loneliness or dating-app-fatigue content with Cuculi’s perspective.

**In-App Leo Message**
Target: Recently signed-up users (last 14 days) who haven’t RSVP’d.
Message: “People are ditching dating apps for group dinners. Curious? Here’s why: [blog link]”

### Rationale

This leverages the second-highest-performing headline archetype from the psychological strategy. Trend pieces have the widest organic reach because they’re shareable beyond your existing user base. The “dating apps” framing taps into a pain point that’s universally understood by your target demographic (late 20s–30s in NYC). Positioning this in Week 2 gives you time to promote and amplify the best-performing social clip with a small paid boost.

### What You’re Testing

- Does trend/cultural content drive more new sign-ups than reactivation content?
- Does LinkedIn perform as a channel for Cuculi’s audience?
- Which subject line framing gets higher open rates: curiosity-gap or declarative statement?

-----

## Week 3: Spring Kickoff — What to Expect (Mar 20–26)

**Theme**: Transparency and anxiety reduction as spring begins and new users explore.
**Primary Audience**: New browsers and first-timers who need clarity, not hype.
**Goal**: Convert consideration-stage users into first RSVPs. Target 5%+ CTA click-through from blog to event page.
**Psychology**: Anxiety reduction + predictability. Remove every unknown that creates hesitation.

### Content Pieces

**Blog (Publish Monday Mar 23)**
_“What Actually Happens at a Cuculi Dinner — From Arrival to Goodbye”_

A minute-by-minute transparency guide (1,000–1,500 words) that walks through the entire experience: what you see when you walk in, how introductions work, what the food situation is like, how long it lasts, what happens if you want to leave early. Written in a warm, FAQ-meets-narrative hybrid format. No hype — just honesty.

- **SEO Keywords**: “what is social dining like,” “Cuculi dinner review,” “first time social dining”
- **Internal Links**: Link to FAQ page, upcoming beginner-friendly tables, and the Week 1 blog for social proof
- **Format**: How-to guide / experience template hybrid

**Newsletter (Send Wednesday Mar 25)**

- Lead: “Going to your first dinner? Here’s everything that happens — no surprises.”
- Featured Events: 3 upcoming “beginner-friendly” tables (smaller group sizes, welcoming hosts, casual restaurants) with host names and short bios
- Segment: New sign-ups (last 30 days) and one-timers
- Subject Line A/B Test: “Your first Cuculi dinner, step by step” vs. “Everything you want to know before your first dinner”

**Social (2 posts — lighter week, quality over quantity)**

1. _Instagram Carousel (Tue)_: “Your first Cuculi dinner in 8 slides.” Each slide covers one moment: the walk-in, the seating, the icebreaker, the food, the conversation, the check, the goodbye, the group chat after. Clean, illustrated or photo-driven.
2. _TikTok (Thu)_: “What actually happens when you show up to dinner with strangers” — walkthrough-style vlog content. Calm, reassuring, no jump cuts.

**In-App Leo Message**
Target: Users who browsed events but didn’t RSVP (last 7 days).
Message: “Wondering what a dinner is actually like? We broke it down minute by minute: [blog link]. And here’s a low-key table near you this week: [event link]”

### Rationale

This maps directly to the “What Actually Happens” headline archetype, which scored highest for anxiety reduction in the psychological strategy. First-timers don’t need more excitement — they need less uncertainty. Spring’s arrival also creates a natural “fresh start” motivation. This is the week where you bridge Q1’s indoor comfort food framing into Q2’s spring energy.

### What You’re Testing

- Does the transparency/guide format convert browsing users into RSVPs better than narrative or trend content?
- Do “beginner-friendly” event labels in the newsletter increase click-through?
- Does a calmer, quieter social content tone (no hype, just information) outperform the energetic formats from Weeks 1–2?

-----

## Week 4: Community Spotlight (Mar 27–31)

**Theme**: Celebrate the people who make Cuculi work — hosts and regulars.
**Primary Audience**: Frequent users and community builders.
**Goal**: Deepen retention and advocacy. Target: social shares, user-generated replies, and repeat RSVP rate.
**Psychology**: Belonging + identity reinforcement. Frequent users see themselves reflected in the community story.

### Content Pieces

**Blog (Publish Monday Mar 30)**
_“Meet the People Who Host Cuculi Dinners — and Why They Keep Coming Back”_

A host spotlight profile (1,000–1,500 words) featuring 2–3 real Cuculi hosts. Covers their backgrounds, why they started hosting, their favorite moments, and what they’ve learned about strangers over shared meals. This is the “community stories” format from the playbook — warm, genuine, not promotional.

- **SEO Keywords**: “host a dinner party NYC,” “social dining host,” “community dinner NYC”
- **Internal Links**: Link to host sign-up page, featured hosts’ upcoming tables
- **Format**: Story/experience template

**Newsletter (Send Wednesday only if Mar 31 falls on a send day — otherwise combine with a “March Wrap-Up” mini-section)**

- Lead: “The people behind your favorite tables.”
- Featured Events: Upcoming tables hosted by the profiled hosts (direct connection between content and action)
- Segment: Active users (attended 2+ events) — reward the engaged base
- Subject Line A/B Test: “The hosts who make it happen” vs. “Why [Host Name] keeps inviting strangers to dinner”

**Social (2 posts — short week)**

1. _Instagram Post (Mon)_: Single host photo + pull-quote from the blog. Clean, human, portrait-style. Encourage tagging and comments.
2. _Twitter/X or Instagram Story (Wed)_: “What’s the best conversation you’ve ever had at a Cuculi dinner?” — community engagement prompt. Collect UGC and testimonials for April content.

**In-App Leo Message**
Target: Users who’ve attended 3+ events.
Message: “You’re part of something. Here’s a look at the hosts who keep showing up — just like you: [blog link]”

### Rationale

The strategy emphasizes that frequent users need deepened bonds, not more activation tactics. Host spotlights serve double duty: they make regulars feel seen while creating aspirational content for newer users who encounter it organically. Ending the month on community rather than acquisition also sets up a natural UGC pipeline for April. The engagement prompt on social creates raw material for future testimonial content.

### What You’re Testing

- Does community/profile content drive higher social shares and engagement than educational or trend content?
- Do personalized host-name subject lines outperform generic framing?
- Does the UGC prompt generate usable testimonial material for next month’s content?

## Monthly Metrics Dashboard

Track these across all four weeks to compare performance by theme, audience, and format.

|Metric                           |Week 1 Target|Week 2 Target|Week 3 Target|Week 4 Target|
|---------------------------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
|Blog Page Views                  |500+         |1,000+       |750+         |500+         |
|Blog → Event Page CTR            |5%+          |3%+          |5%+          |4%+          |
|Newsletter Open Rate             |25%+         |25%+         |25%+         |30%+         |
|Newsletter CTR to Tables         |5%+          |4%+          |5%+          |5%+          |
|Social Engagement Rate           |4%+          |5%+          |3%+          |5%+          |
|New RSVPs (content-attributed)   |15+          |25+          |20+          |10+          |
|New Sign-Ups (content-attributed)|—            |50+          |20+          |—            |

**Why targets vary by week**: Week 1 targets a small segment (inactive) so volume is lower but conversion should be higher. Week 2 is the big awareness play, so reach matters more than per-user conversion. Week 3 bridges consideration to action with the highest conversion intent. Week 4 prioritizes engagement depth over volume.

-----

## End-of-Month Review Framework

At the end of March, answer these five questions to shape April’s calendar:

1. **Which blog format drove the highest event-page click-through?** (First-person narrative vs. trend piece vs. transparency guide vs. host profile) — This determines your default format going forward.
2. **Which audience segment was most responsive to content?** (Inactive vs. prospective vs. first-timers vs. regulars) — This tells you where to double down and where to experiment further.
3. **Which social platform and format generated the most engagement per post?** — This shapes your 2–3 clips/week allocation in April.
4. **Did newsletter A/B test winners share a pattern?** (Curiosity-gap vs. declarative, personalized vs. generic) — This informs your subject line playbook.
5. **What content can be repurposed for April?** — The Week 4 UGC prompt, top-performing social clips for paid boosting, and any blog that over-performed for a refresh or sequel.

-----

## Production Notes

- **Blog publish day**: Monday (gives the full week for repurposing and newsletter distribution)
- **Newsletter send day**: Wednesday (mid-week, optimized for open rates per industry benchmarks)
- **Social cadence**: 2–3 posts/week, staggered Tue/Thu/Fri or Mon/Wed depending on the week
- **Leo in-app messages**: Triggered after blog publish, targeted by segment, capped at one message per user per week
- **Paid boost budget**: Reserve for Week 2’s best-performing social clip only — light, disciplined spend on the content with the widest organic signal
- **Image assets needed**: 4 blog featured images (1200x630), 2 carousel sets, 2 short-form video shoots or edits, 1 host portrait photo
